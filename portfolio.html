    Andrei Tarkovsky's Stalker

    "I used to say this all the time, when people... back when Russia was the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics... and they'd say 'oh aren't you so glad that you're in America?' and I'd say 'Well I know a lot of Russian filmmakers. They have a lot more freedom than I have." -George Lucas

    Whenever I think of this film, I inexorably think of this quote from George Lucas. Lucas wasn't speaking about personal freedom, or political freedom, or even freedom of speech. Lucas was strictly referring to the constraints of capital. For the average American, it's nearly impossible to disconnect the propagandized vision of Soviet social standards from reality, but no film greater exemplifies the true artistic freedom soviet filmmakers enjoyed than Tarkovsky's Stalker. It doesn't take a Tankie to see that art and science flourished under soviet rule. And it doesn't take a neoliberal to see at which the incredible cost of life this was purchased at under Stalinism. But it does take an informed and nuanced approach to understand the complex structures that controlled both American and Soviet art.

    Mark Fisher's Capitalist Realism partly poses the plot of Children of Men as allegory for the philosophical anxiety that comes from the lack of "new". To quote: "Children of Men connects with the suspicion that the end has already come, the thought that it could well be the case that the future harbor only reinteration and re-permutation. Could it be that there are no breaks, no `shocks of the new` to come?" Fisher lived long enough to see the homogenization of Hollywood in a near preminatory fashion(Albiet the idea of capital casting out novel in favor of recognition was not pioneered by him). The interview in which I quoted Lucas took place in 2015, and even then the average American moviegoer was already torn on the fatigue coming from the constant stream of recycled IP's. "Everything is a remake, a reboot, or a sequel these days". A common sentiment that transcends income, education, philosophy, and politics. In Hollywood, original scripts are seen as risky. Studios want to mitigate as much risk as possible, because they are beholden to investors or a board of directors. Those powers are investing money in hopes that it turns into more money. The control that comes from capital investment trickles back down that chain and removes the risk. New ideas are forced into familiar shapes. The Cloverfield universe films were rewritten so poorly to fit into their Cloverfield shaped box, it gives a wonderful look behind the scenes at what happens to "the new" today. Stalker could only ever exist in Soviet Russia. Everything that went into the film; the material conditions to the sociopolitical environment. The window in which a film would even approach this is rapidly closing, and might have already closed.

    Loosly based on the novel Roadside Picnic by authors Arkady and Boris Strugatsky, Stalker is about a man whom works in an unnamed location as a "Stalker" leading people through the "Zone", an area in which the normal laws of physics do not apply and remnants of seemingly extraterrestrial activity lie undisturbed among its ruins. The Zone contains a place called the "Room", said to grant the wishes of anyone who steps inside. The area containing the Zone is shrouded in secrecy, sealed off by the government and surrounded by ominous hazards. The Stalker guides 2 men through the military checkpoint guarding the entrance, then the open and dangerous wilderness of the Zone, to the Room. Along the way, they discuss motivation, ambition, fears, and life. Like a fractal, a seemly simple plot and theming, that upon closer inspection is infinitely complex, so much so that deep analysis is somewhat futile, yet as a critic we are drawn to do.
